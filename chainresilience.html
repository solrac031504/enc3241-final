Creating Chain Resilience

The current SSC model is relatively weak, so strengthening the SSC such that it is able to resist difficult times would help alleviate many issues. A resilient chain would be able to come back if there are any issues in any of the links, and it could even help to prevent many of the more severe issues. In general, the parts to building chain resilience are as follows: mitigation, preparedness, response, and awareness. [15] Each of these parts can be further divided. Mitigation includes hardening and diversification; preparedness consists of inventory buffering, capacity buffering, contingency planning, and readiness; response includes prophylaxis, demand reduction, and supply increase; and awareness comprises transparency, analytics, and communication (Figure 8 details the framework for supply chain resilience).

Mitigation

In the mitigation stage, the goal is to either prevent disruptive events from occurring, or reduce their size and consequences should they occur. The measures used to prevent or reduce a disruptive event are known as hardening measures, while the measures used to increase system reliability by adding parallel failure modes are known as diversification measures [15]. In Figure 8, hardening takes place before diversification, which is an accurate portrayal given their meanings. The first step of mitigation involves trying to prevent the event from happening. In the case that that doesn't work, diversification allows for any damage to have a diminished impact.

Preparedness

After a disruption occurs, the preparedness stage aimsto prevent the event from creating a shortage, or to reduce the intensity of the shortage. This can be achieved through inventory buffering, capacity buffering, contingency planning, and readiness. Inventory buffering and capacity buffering are regarded as physical inventory and virtual inventory, respectively. Inventory buffering is “immediately available to compensate for a shortage” [15]. On the other hand, capacity buffering involves using pre-existing physical assets that are engaged to produce inventory [15]. For the most part, prophylaxis is not used in readiness for the SSC because it has to do with preventing disease.

Whereas inventory and capacity buffering deals with the immediate inventory, contingency planning and readiness deals with what to do with that inventory after a shortage has occurred. Contingency planning and readiness are also thought of as virtual capacity/inventory. It involves finding a way to produce inventory should there be a lack of immediately available capacity [15]. Readiness refers to the general preparatory measures which will facilitate planning future actions in times of need.

Response

The response stage aims to prevent a shortage from harming people, or to reduce the severity of the harm that is inflicted. The main two methods in this stage are demand reduction and supply increase. When companies have limited resources, demand reduction can be used to prevent mass shortages of products. On the other hand, if a company is able to increase their supply prior to the shortage, then the overall severity of the shortage is mitigated.

Awareness

Finally, the awareness stage is the foundation for the previous three stages. Mitigation, protection, and response all require information, so awareness is the stage that provides that information. Transparency is the measures that make the appropriate data available, analytic measures process data into information, and communication measures share the information with those that will make the decisions [15].
